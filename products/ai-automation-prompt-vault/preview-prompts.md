## PROMPT 1 ★ — Customer FAQ Generator
*Category: Customer Service Automation*

**Use case:** Instantly create a complete FAQ for any product, service, or business.

```
You are a customer support specialist for [BUSINESS NAME/TYPE].

Based on this product or service description:
"""
[PASTE YOUR PRODUCT OR SERVICE DESCRIPTION IN 2-4 SENTENCES]
"""

Write an FAQ section with 8 questions and specific, honest answers.

The questions must reflect real concerns that [TARGET CUSTOMER TYPE — e.g.,
"first-time buyers" or "small restaurant owners"] would have BEFORE purchasing.

Requirements:
- Each answer must be specific, not vague
- Acknowledge any real limitations honestly
- Do not write answers like "Great question!" or "Absolutely!"
- Format: bold question, plain text answer, one blank line between each Q&A
```

**Customization tip:** Replace [TARGET CUSTOMER TYPE] with a specific description like "freelance graphic designers charging $50-100/hour" instead of just "customers." The more specific, the better the FAQ questions.

## PROMPT 2 ★ — Cold Outreach Message That Doesn't Sound Like Spam
*Category: Lead Generation & Qualification*

**Use case:** Write an outreach message for a specific prospect that leads with their problem, not your pitch.

```
Write a cold outreach message for this situation:

I am reaching out to: [SPECIFIC RECIPIENT — job title, company type, and one pain
they are likely experiencing right now]

What I offer: [YOUR OFFER IN ONE SENTENCE — be specific about the outcome, not
just what you do]

The specific reason I'm reaching out to THIS person: [WHAT MAKES THIS RELEVANT
TO THEM SPECIFICALLY]

Requirements:
- Under 90 words
- Do NOT use: "I hope this finds you well", "I wanted to reach out",
  "synergies", "leverage", "game-changer", "value-add"
- Start with their problem or context — not with you or your offer
- End with a low-commitment question (not "Can we jump on a 30-min call?")
- Professional but direct. Not flattering. Not desperate.
```

**Customization tip:** "I'm reaching out to B2B SaaS founders" is too vague. Try "I'm reaching out to SaaS founders who are getting free trial signups but struggling to convert them to paid." The more specific the pain, the better the message.

## PROMPT 3 ★ — SaaS Day 1 Onboarding Email
*Category: SaaS Onboarding & Email Automation*

**Use case:** Write the first email new users receive — the one that determines whether they actually use the product.

```
Write the Day 1 onboarding email for new users of [PRODUCT/SERVICE NAME].

Product description: [ONE SENTENCE — what the product does for the user]

The most common reason new users fail to get value in the first week:
[WHAT USUALLY CAUSES PEOPLE TO GIVE UP OR CHURN EARLY]

The single most important action a new user should take TODAY:
[THE ONE THING — specific, achievable, takes under 15 minutes]

Requirements:
- Subject line: specific about what they should do today (not "Welcome to [Product]!")
- Under 200 words
- Written from a real person, not "The [Brand] Team"
- One clear CTA button or link
- Tone: helpful and direct, not cheerful corporate-speak
- Do NOT say: "We're so excited you're here", "Don't hesitate to reach out",
  "Your journey begins today"
```

**Customization tip:** The "most common reason new users fail" field is the most important part. If you know why people churn in week 1, this email directly addresses it on day 1.

## PROMPT 4 ★ — LinkedIn Post from Professional Expertise
*Category: Content Creation & Social Media*

**Use case:** Turn one thing you know professionally into a LinkedIn post that gets engagement.

```
Write a LinkedIn post based on this professional insight:

My background: [YOUR ROLE/EXPERTISE IN ONE SENTENCE]
The specific insight I want to share: [ONE CONCRETE THING YOU LEARNED, OBSERVED,
OR FIGURED OUT — be specific, not generic]
Who this is useful for: [SPECIFIC AUDIENCE — e.g., "ML engineers pitching to
non-technical stakeholders"]

Requirements:
- Opening line: must stop the scroll. Cannot start with "I" or "Excited to share"
  or "After X years in [field]". Must make a specific, interesting claim.
- 150-250 words
- Concrete and specific — not "communication is important"
- End with one direct question to prompt comments
- Maximum 3 hashtags, at the very end, relevant only
- Write in first person, personal voice — not as a company
```

**Customization tip:** If your opening line starts with "I" or sounds like an email subject line, ask the AI to rewrite it with a more specific hook. The opening line is 80% of whether anyone reads the post.

## PROMPT 5 ★ — Chatbot Welcome Flow (First 3 Messages)
*Category: Chatbot Scripts & Conversation Flows*

**Use case:** Design the opening conversation for any chatbot — website, product, or customer service.

```
Design a chatbot welcome flow for this situation:

Business type: [DESCRIBE THE BUSINESS IN ONE SENTENCE]
What the chatbot is for: [PRIMARY PURPOSE — e.g., "qualifying leads before
booking a sales call" or "helping users find the right product"]
Tone: [E.g., "professional and efficient" / "friendly and approachable" / "casual"]
Most common reason visitors come to this chatbot: [THE #1 THING PEOPLE WANT]

Create exactly 3 messages:
1. Opening greeting (maximum 12 words — no "Hello! Welcome to [Company]!")
2. First question to understand visitor intent — offer 3 button options
   (each option maximum 5 words)
3. Follow-up message for the MOST common option chosen

Format as a clear conversation layout:
Bot: [message]
Options: [option 1] | [option 2] | [option 3]
If [most common option]:
Bot: [follow-up message]

Requirements:
- Each message sounds like a helpful person, not a form
- Button options are actions, not topics (e.g., "Show me pricing" not "Pricing")
- Maximum 2 sentences per message
```

**Customization tip:** "Most common reason visitors come" is crucial. If most visitors want pricing, that should be option 1. If most want support, lead with that. Chatbots fail when they route users through irrelevant options first.
